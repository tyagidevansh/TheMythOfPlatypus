Ever stare at a blank wall, heart pounding, thoughts racing over an uncertain sense of reality? This fear of the unknown strips away the freedom of choice and the feeling of control. Imagine being stranded at sea with no sense of direction or time the feeling of being lost. This is Angst.

Angst, or _anxiety_ as defined by the Danish philosopher Søren Kierkegaard, is often associated with existentialism, a movement emphasizing individual freedom. Existentialists believe that we are responsible for creating our own meaning in life.

Kierkegaard describes Angst as the "dizziness of freedom."

As we navigate life, we constantly face choices that lead to different outcomes. The fear of making the wrong choice can be paralyzing, leading to anxiety and despair. It arises from the realization that although we are free to choose, we cannot always predict or control the consequences.

Is there a way to always make the correct decision? We are exposed to nearly infinite unknown possibilities that become reality only through our choices and actions.

We are driven by anxiety to make choices that eliminate it, but alas, we are savages chasing our tails, running in circles. A man dying of thirst may choose to drink salt water, which only worsens his condition. He may choose to chase a mirage _(mentioning Mirage makes me miss CS:GO)_. The freedom to choose and do anything can sometimes keep us from doing anything at all.

> “Just as a physician might say that there is very likely not one single living human being who is completely healthy, so anyone who really knows mankind might say there is not one single living human being who does not secretly harbor an unrest, an inner strife, a disharmony, an anxiety about an unknown something or something he does not even dare to try to know, an anxiety about some possibility in existence or an anxiety about himself an anxiety he cannot explain.”
>
> — **Søren Kierkegaard**

Part of the reason I am fascinated by Kierkegaard's tale of angst is how he perceives it. He sought to understand and embrace angst, rather than trying to eliminate it. He believed that angst is an essential part of the human experience.

Without any possibility, there would be no anxiety, but without anxiety, there would be no possibility.

He believed the response to anxiety is what makes the difference between a life of despair and a life of passion. To achieve a life filled with passion, we must not give in. Using this anxiety as a source will allow us to develop a deeper understanding of ourselves and the world around us. It becomes a catalyst for growth and self discovery.

Kierkegaard suggested that to make use of anxiety, we must pursue a "passion." This could take many forms, such as a creative pursuit, a cause we care about, a relationship, an ideology, or a belief. By channeling our anxiety into something meaningful, we can find purpose and fulfillment.

He believed that one should orient their passion based on Subjective Truth something that isn't just intellectualization but also an emotional, subjective experience.

> "What I really need is to get clear about what I must do, not what I must know, except insofar as knowledge must precede every act. What matters is to find a purpose, to see what it really is that God wills that I shall do; the crucial thing is to find a truth which is truth for me, to find the idea for which I am willing to live and die."
>
> — **Søren Kierkegaard**

According to Kierkegaard, an individual must discover their subjective truth and then throw their weight commit fully in its direction, beyond reason, beyond logic, beyond intellect. To do something for the sake of itself is to take a leap of faith. _(Kierkegaard did it before Spider-Man)._

For Kierkegaard, inaction and giving in to anxiety results in a depressed, apathetic state a failed, lost, and unrealized self.

Nowadays, anxiety is seen as something wrong and negative, however, it is a fundamental part of what makes us human. To feel and to think is to be anxious. We must, like Kierkegaard suggested, try our best to learn to live through it, to keep moving forward into the unknown, and take our leap of faith.
