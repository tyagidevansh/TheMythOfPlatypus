It is quite rare that I feel fulfilled at the end of a day. I’m not depressed by any means, in fact I’d like to claim that I am well below average in my level of perceived sadness. I say this because I know how desperate I get when I start feeling down, I try to do whatever I can to get out of the rut I find myself in every few weeks. It’s quite rare that I feel sad. At the same time, it’s quite rare that I feel happy.

Absence of sadness is not happiness. When you’re sad you don’t wish to get out of bed because there’s nothing to look forward to. When you’re not sad, you get out of bed without any particular difficulty and start doing whatever you’re supposed to. However, when you’re happy you wake up with a smile. You don’t roll out of bed, you jump out of it. At the risk of sounding cliché, the world just seems wonderful some days. The sky you see everyday looks bluer; the sunlight feels comforting and each breath has a hint of sweetness to. I describe this state in such rosy terms because I crave it, and I don’t get to experience it often. 

Everyone experiences happiness differently, and naturally there is no single “10-step guide to feeling elated”. I can only talk about my experiences and the observations I have been making about myself over the years reveal one key thing: I am happy when I feel like I am doing something meaningful. It would stand to reason, then, if I am not feeling happy, I must not be doing meaningful work (refer to the formal proof below).

![Predicte logic](/assets/doing-nothing/proof.png)

I included the proof as a joke, but if you look at it carefully, you’ll notice an important word – “believe”. In order to feel happy, I don’t have to be doing meaningful work, I just need to believe that it is meaningful. You might be thinking that I am going to share an epiphany on “how to trick your brain to think you’re always being productive”, but alas, that is not the case. The unfortunate reality of the reliance on perception of meaningfulness (is there an objective measure of meaningfulness as it relates to work?) is that quite often I end up believing that the meaningful work I do isn’t meaningful.
Let’s carry on the structured theme introduced by predicate logic and define 4 kinds of tasks based on the perception vs reality of meaningfulness:
- **Tasks which are meaningful and feel meaningful.** For example, writing a blog, going for a run or solving a challenging leetcode problem. There is very little disconnect between perception and reality here. I know solving writing is good for my brain, and I feel happy once I publish a blog. Even then, these tasks are hard. They’re voluntary. No one’s holding me to them, and it’s quite easy to fall back to all the other types of tasks. 
- **Tasks which are meaningful but don’t feel meaningful.** For example, prompting Claude at work all day. This task drains me and just feels like busy work. I feel like I learn nothing from it, but employment is objectively meaningful. Even looking at it from a purely educational perspective, it feels like I learn nothing from vibecoding at work, but I do. Logically I know I am much better at my job today than 6 months ago, but I don’t feel like I’ve gotten smarter. Type 2 tasks are defined by this disconnect between perception and reality.  
- **Tasks which aren’t meaningful but feel meaningful.** These are the tasks which are done voluntarily, and feel like they’re making me a better person, but more often than not they’re a complete waste of time. There is some overlap between Type 3 and type 1 tasks. Depending on the context, tasks can switch from type 1 to 3 and vice versa. Watching a programming YouTube video is a type 3 task. Most of the time I retain nothing from them, and the information I do retain isn’t valuable. However, the bigger issue is that type 3 tasks lull my brain into a false sense of achievement. Passively reading a CS book does NOTHING. It does not teach me anything, yet reading a book is so much easier than actually writing code. Writing code is hard, feeling like you’re learning to write code isn’t. A challenging leetcode problem is a type 1 task, but an easy leetcode problem (or solving leetcode problems by copying the solutions) is a type 3 task. These tasks are dangerous as they neither give rest to the brain, nor do they put it to work. Waking up my brain from a type 3 task induced coma is really challenging.
- **Tasks which aren’t meaningful and don’t feel meaningful.** These are the activities that I just enjoy doing. I know they won’t make me a better engineer or a better person, but I need these activities to do all the other things. All work and no play makes Devansh a dull boy or something. Watching a good YouTube video or reading a fun manga is a type 4 activity. I do not need to explain why type 4 activities need to be strictly limited either way.

One half of the answer to the question posed in the title is this: I don’t feel like I ever do anything because I don’t do enough type 1 tasks. This is true, but it’s not the full picture. I must also now answer why type 1 tasks are so special. I think it’s because none of the other tasks accumulate. 

Type 2 tasks are also productive, but they don’t accumulate. I work for several days and ship a feature that allows the content team to automatically place new quizzes into existing courses by specifying the video details for the quizzes. Is this meaningful work? Sure. By performing this task I earned some money, and I made the lives of the content team slightly simpler. However, will I remember all the hours I spent on this particular problem? No.

The reason is simple - once the task is done, it disappears. Once I watch an Instagram reel, it disappears. Once I watch a programming tutorial, it disappears. However, the blog I write remains. Pogberry remains. Ally remains. Type 1 tasks stack. They leave me with learnings I remember, and the challenges I faced while performing those tasks stay with me because the final product is always in front of me.

Persistent memories of challenges overcome with great hardship define meaningful tasks. 

Perhaps this definition applies to no one other than me. Perhaps this need for a constant reminder of “you are not worthless, look at the things you’ve made” is why I feel the urge to log every productive minute on Ally. Perhaps that’s why I always compare everything I’ve made with all the amazing things that exist in the world and feel inadequate. I need to create more, because I do not have enough memories of the good things I have done. 

For a task to be meaningful, its memory must not be ephemeral. I do not remember what I did at work 4 months ago, but I remember writing Raylib bindings for Pogberry 11 months ago. I want to learn an instrument because I wish to remember all the days I spent learning it once I’m decent enough to play. Every practice session would be rewarding, as I would have something to look forward to as well as something to look back on. I would know the task I’m doing will carry value for me far into the future. 
I prefer reading books in paperback, not because I dislike reading on a screen, but because I wish to have physical proof that I did something with my time. If I hadn’t picked up electronics earlier this year, I’d have nothing to remember January by. When I was making my silly circuits, I was happy – not just because I was genuinely enjoying learning about them, but also because I knew this is something I’d remember for a long time. 

I always feel like I do nothing with my time because most things I do stop existing as soon as I complete them. A YouTube video disappears as soon as I finish it, but anything I do because I really wanted to do it stays in my mind, forever. But the things that I want to do take a lot of effort to do, and hence I’d rather just do something else. But that other thing leaves no lasting impact and I’m back to the starting point of being unhappy. This blog is my way of accepting this circular relationship. Perhaps I can slow down this wheel of unhappiness. Perhaps I can bring it a stop. Perhaps I can break the wheel.
