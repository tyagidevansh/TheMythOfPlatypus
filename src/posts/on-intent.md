Remember how long you've put things off, remember how many times you've uttered the phrase "another day." Does that fill you with a sense of guilt? What if I ask you to think about the time that you've spent consuming media in the past week? How many times did you say "not now" to YouTube or to Twitter? I hope the guilt's rising in your chest, because I'm here to argue you need not necessarily feel that way. The deciding factor in whether something is "good" for you or "bad" for you is not some grand sense of right and wrong; your actions, I believe, should only be dictated by Intent.

Intent is easy to define; it is simply the state of wanting to do something. To be determined to achieve a goal is to have Intent. What is important to clarify, however, is the difference between doing something because you _must_ do it versus doing something because you _want_ to do it.

There is no free will when it comes to exams, for me at least (I know one certain reader of this blog somehow manages to practice free will through every exam, but I, unfortunately, am not yet so enlightened). You _must_ study for exams, no other way around it. This action requires very little Intent, and I would label this task easy. Studying for exams with the express purpose of scoring much more than you require to satisfy the expectations of those around you requires Intent. That is a task I would label hard, and a task that would bring you happiness regardless of outcome.

Being happy is my life's ultimate goal. Is it everyone's ultimate goal? Is it even a worthwhile goal? I'm not convinced. In either case, I do not desire a happiness that is shallow. Anything that gives you joy only while you experience it is worthless.

And here, my friends, this article's grand proclamation: everything you do with Intent will give you happiness.

Do not fret over if something is good for you or not, only ask yourself one question: **Do I WANT to do this?** If the answer is "maybe," stay away from it. No exceptions.

Going back to our silly example, if you study for an exam because you are obliged to, will you ever find joy? Will the act of studying ever excite you? Do you even care for the outcome? No, no you do not.

You tell yourself to study, and you do study. You have all the discipline in the world, and you even do well in that exam. But will you ever look back on it fondly? Will you ever _miss_ the act of preparation? I don't think so. In this situation the control over your life lies not with you, but with the intangible "obligations."

Now what if you swap this sleepwalking with something with more Intent?

Of course, obligations never go away. I'm too young to have many of them, but one day I'm certain they will pile on so high you might as well look at the collection and call it a mountain. That mountain must be climbed, it is true, but how about we make the peak taller?

On top of the mountain-pile of obligations, how about we add a few shovelfuls of goals? Look up at the new, taller peak now, and start climbing. Whenever you climb anything, your gaze is fixed at the top, correct? Even when walking on a straight path you look towards the end, your eyes do not randomly fixate on a point 80% from the end.

This makes all the difference in my opinion. Once you set your _own goals_ on top of what you must do, the bulk of the work becomes an afterthought. If you're mentally prepared to reach the top, the bottom 80% will occupy a shockingly little part of your brain. Add to it the fact that you will be filled with the self-satisfaction that you did it because you _wanted_ to do it.

I'm not prescribing that everyone must do more, or heck, even do stuff that is good for them. All I preach is to do what you must do with a sense of indifference, because the mountain is taller yet, and you must reach the top.

If you wish to complain, complain about the height you've added to the top. This extra height can be made up of whatever you desire. "I want to score more than a certain friend," "I want to spend more time playing games," "I want to become stronger" - all is fair and all is respectable.

What you decide to add to make your life fuller of Intent does not matter as long as you remember to add material to the top of the mountain, rather than simply throwing it around the base.

**Make the mountain taller, not wider.**

Once the mountain has grown taller, and what you desire lies at the top, what option do you have other than to walk up the drab bits first? I'd bet the view is prettier up there too. No one's asking you to beeline to the top either. This isn't a climbing competition. Take the windier route, who cares? If you want to doomscroll, do it, but do it because you wish to do it.

If you really want to do something, don't hold yourself back even if common knowledge is that it's not a good use of your time. I trust you and your judgement, if you truly want to do something, it cannot be that bad for you.

Watch reels, but remember to do it while being present in the moment. If you scroll reels and at the end of 20 minutes you look up and wonder why you've spent all the time staring at a screen, you've failed. Similarly, if you spend 5 hours working but at the end you cannot recall what decisions you made and why, you've failed too. If you're watching a movie, it better not be just to fill time. Life's too valuable for filler.

On the flipside, even if you do not wish to do something but it is something that you really must do, convince yourself that you really want it.

This "must" may seem to be going against the message I have so far been talking about, but remember the mountain climb may have some ravines, and sooner or later you're going to have to cross them. Remember, not even the gods have the power of coercion over us. The only one who controls your desires is you.

Gaslight yourself, but do not do anything without intending to do it. Keep your eyes open even if you'd rather let your eyelids fall and your steps be guided by an invisible hand. No matter how soft the hand may be, let go of it and rely on nothing else apart from your eyes; even if they stare at darkness, tell them to imagine a path forward.

Make your mountain taller, live with Intent and you will be equally happy through the pleasant strolls and the arduous falls, through the rewarding peaks and the gutting ravines. If you do not let the peak out of your sight, the peak of your own creation that you so desperately wish to reach, even the muddiest paths will not break your spirit.
