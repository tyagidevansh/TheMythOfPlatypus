The winds surrounding Kubrea were calm that day. The Dark Sea wasn't spitting its wrath over the shores of the small island. The sun was out and the skies were blue.

All of this was deeply unsettling.

The sea lay strangely still beneath the cliffs. Not smooth, for the Dark Sea was never smooth, but subdued. The waves arrived in long, tired rolls, as though they had travelled an impossible distance and forgotten what they had come for. They struck the black rocks without conviction. The spray rose only a few feet before drifting back into the water.

This was regular behaviour for a sea, but the Dark Sea was no ordinary sea. It had been cursed to extract eternal vengeance. Words describing tranquillity did not find a place in the scrolls carrying its fate, yet that day those waters could be described as tranquil. Each pair of eyes which fell upon its surface asked the same question – “What does this mean?”

The old men sitting outside the harbor tavern watched the horizon in silence. No one remarked upon the weather. It did not feel real enough to comment on. Putting the weather into words might have broken the illusion they were surely under, for Kubrea could not have been blessed with the warmth their skins were feeling.

Children played in the streets below them. Women carried baskets between market stalls. Fishermen repaired nets on the docks. Life continued as it always had. Yet every so often someone would pause in the middle of a task and glance westward. Then they would quickly return to what they had been doing, as though they had seen something they did not wish to see twice.

Kubrea had never been a cheerful place. The island sat at the edge of the known world, where the ocean widened into an endless expanse of dark water and storm. Every settlement on its western coast had been built as though expecting a siege. Houses crouched low to the ground behind thick stone walls. Roofs were held down by iron chains. Narrow streets wound between buildings not for beauty but for shelter, bending and twisting to break the force of the wind. The buildings all followed the same rules: no windows facing west, no doors facing west. Only walls. The storm had taught those lessons generations ago.

On every day prior to that fateful day the wind was present. On some days it was a persistent push, enough to keep any ships from sailing west, but not strong enough to get past the two walls and the ruins of a third wall on the cliffs at the edge of the land. On other days, the winds blew fast enough to lift the seawater over the massive rockface which usually separated it from the pastures above. Stronger winds had never been felt any place east of Kubream, the settlers could only imagine the hellscape which lay westwards. No one in a hundred years had ever dared to set sail on the Dark Sea, even on days of relative calm. The Veil was visible, and it was a deity to be feared, not approached.

No one remembered when the storms had begun. The first settlers who landed on Kubrea had written of them. The kings who followed them had written of them. The empire whose maps stretched across half the world had written of them too.
Every account described the same thing. Clouds gathering upon the western horizon, thunder rolling over the sea, winds that never fully rested. Beyond that horizon, hundreds of miles from Kubrea's shores, lay the Great Storm.

There were many names for it. The Wall. The Veil. The Sleeping God. The Last Horizon. It was an eternal God of no mythology, worshipped even by the Kings of Etharia. But the stories surrounding it changed far more than the storm itself. No one knew where the storm began, or what the world looked like on the other side of the storm. Many believed the storm to be the end of the world, though the mathematicians were certain the world was much larger than all the great lands combined.

Beyond the storm lay uncertainty. Whatever the truth, humanity had never crossed the Dark Sea. Not many dreamt of it either, the matter had been settled ages ago. Entire fleets had vanished attempting the voyage. Every sailor knew the Dark Sea was too dangerous to set off on, and that was unquestionable knowledge.

On Kubrea, where the storm's distant breath could be felt everyday, people had developed habits they never questioned. They slept better when rain hammered their roofs and found comfort in thunder. Many could judge the hour of day and the time of year simply by listening to the wind.

The absence of those things left a hollow feeling, like entering a familiar room and finding the furniture moved during the night.

By midday, the western cliffs had grown busier than usual. The horizon was visible that day - there was a sharp line dividing the sea and the sky. It was hard to believe; many did not believe the horizon existed to the west of Kubrea. They had only ever seen an unclear expanse of grey stretching out as far as they could see, with no way to know where the grey sea ended and where the grey sky began. Even when it was possible to stand alongside the ruins of the Old Wall right by the drop, or climb up the Salt Wall, even when the skies above Kubrea lightened enough that one could make out the Sun at noon, the skies over to the west stayed foreboding. They never let up.

Several fishermen later swore that they had never seen the horizon so clearly before. Others claimed they had. Arguments followed. The fishermen were the only ones who ever dared to sail around the island from the harbour on the east shore. The never went far west, but on days when the wind lets up, the quality of the catch on the western shores was worth the risk. Kubrea existed for its excellent fish and sheep after all.

As evening approached, golden light spread across the water. The cliffs glowed amber and the sea reflected the sky. That day, Kubrea was beautiful. For the first time in all their lives, it felt to the settlers that their homeland wasn’t trying to kick them out. The sun gently dived into the Dark Sea for the first time ever, and the Dark Sea wasn’t dark anymore. It burned red, and the sight made dozens burst into tears.

The island had known calm days before. Not many, but enough. The island had known clear skies. The island had known sunlight. Yet beneath every memory lingered another memory: the certainty that the wind would return by nightfall. The certainty that rain would sweep in from the west. The certainty that the world remained as it had always been.

That certainty faded away that day, for the sea to the west of Kubrea felt the sunlight glisten on top of it for the first time in millennia.

As darkness fell, lanterns appeared throughout the town. People retired to their homes. Doors were barred and windows were shut just like any other day. The wind’s strength is to never be underestimated. You must always never take your armour off when facing off against it. But that day, everyone knew the armour plates were unnecessary weight.

All across Kubrea, countless men and women found themselves lying awake long after midnight.

Listening.

Waiting.

For thunder that never came.
